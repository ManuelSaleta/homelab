Switching from a public Cloudflare Tunnel to **Tailscale** for your sensitive, internal-only apps is an excellent architectural pivot.

While Cloudflare Tunnel is perfect for public web apps (like your Homepage or Pi-hole admin dashboard), it still exposes those endpoints to the public internet edge. For a NAS or your personal Obsidian notes vault, you want **Zero Public Exposure**.

Tailscale creates an encrypted, point-to-point WireGuard mesh network (a "Tailnet"). Your devices talk directly to each other over an encrypted tunnel, meaning your NAS is accessible *only* when your workstation or phone has its Tailscale client turned on—completely hidden from the rest of the world.

To keep this strictly aligned with your declarative, infrastructure-as-code cattle philosophy, here is how you can implement this.

---

### The Architecture Options

You have two clean ways to bake Tailscale into your homelab depending on where your NAS lives:

1. **Natively on the Proxmox Host (Best if your NAS is an LXC/VM/ZFS pool on the hardware):** You install the Tailscale daemon directly on `mothership` or inside the specific storage VM, allowing you to access the raw network storage protocols (like SMB, NFS, or SSH) securely from anywhere.
2. **Inside K3s as a Subnet Router / Ingress Proxy (Best if you want to route into cluster-only services):** You deploy a lightweight Tailscale pod inside your `networking` namespace that acts as a secure gateway into your cluster's private IPs.

Let's look at setting up the **K3s Subnet Router & Proxy**, as it allows you to hit any internal cluster service securely without exposing it via Cloudflare.

---

### Step 1: Generate an Auth Key

1. Log into your **Tailscale Admin Console**.
2. Navigate to **Settings** -> **Keys**.
3. Click **Generate auth key**.
4. Check **Reusable** (so if the pod restarts, it can reconnect) and click **Generate**. Copy the key (`tskey-auth-...`).

---

### Step 2: Deploy Tailscale Declaratively to K3s

Instead of messing around with manual container configurations, we can deploy Tailscale directly into your `kubernetes/infrastructure/` layer.

First, store your authentication token securely as a Kubernetes Secret:

```bash
kubectl create secret generic tailscale-secret \
  -n networking \
  --from-literal=TS_AUTHKEY="tskey-auth-YOUR-KEY-HERE"

```

Next, create `kubernetes/infrastructure/tailscale-router.yaml`:

```yaml
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: tailscale-subnet-router
  namespace: networking
  labels:
    app: tailscale
spec:
  replicas: 1
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: tailscale
  template:
    metadata:
      labels:
        app: tailscale
    spec:
      serviceAccountName: default
      containers:
      - name: tailscale
        image: tailscale/tailscale:latest
        imagePullPolicy: IfNotPresent
        env:
        - name: TS_AUTHKEY
          valueFrom:
            secretKeyRef:
              name: tailscale-secret
              key: TS_AUTHKEY
        # This tells Tailscale to expose your home LAN and K3s Service/Pod IP networks
        - name: TS_ROUTES
          value: "192.168.50.0/24,10.42.0.0/16" 
        - name: TS_USERSPACE
          value: "false"
        securityContext:
          capabilities:
            add:
            - NET_ADMIN # Required for WireGuard routing tricks
        resources:
          requests:
            cpu: "50m"
            memory: "64Mi"
          limits:
            cpu: "200m"
            memory: "128Mi"

```

Apply it to the cluster:

```bash
kubectl apply -f kubernetes/infrastructure/tailscale-router.yaml

```

---

### Step 3: Approve the Subnet Routes

Once the pod spins up, look at your Tailscale Admin Console. You will see a new machine check in named `tailscale-subnet-router`.

1. Click the **three dots** next to the new machine.
2. Select **Edit route settings**.
3. Under **Subnet routes**, toggle on the checkbox for your home network (`192.168.50.0/24`) and your cluster subnet (`10.42.0.0/16`).

### 💡 Managing Personal Notes (Obsidian Sync)

Since you use Obsidian for your note-taking vault, pairing this Tailscale setup with a self-hosted storage service inside your cluster opens up incredible automation:

* **The Setup:** You can deploy a lightweight self-hosted storage container (like **CouchDB** or an **S3-compatible MinIO instance**) into K3s, or point straight to an isolated SMB share on your NAS.
* **The Routine:** Instead of exposing that storage engine via Traefik to a public `notes.freesalty.com` domain, you leave it with **no Ingress manifest at all**. It stays accessible *only* to the local network.
* **The Magic:** When you are traveling or away from home, you just toggle Tailscale on your mobile phone or laptop. Obsidian can seamlessly sync your markdown files straight to your internal storage server over the encrypted WireGuard link as if you were sitting right at your desk, with absolute cryptographic isolation from the public internet.
